GI Synergy Ingredients

A line-by-line look at what's inside Apex Energetics GI Synergy (K-64), including active components and excipients.

GI Synergy is three bottles pulling together, not a single capsule, and each one has a part to play. The herbs listed below match the current pack; the doses are clinical and track the published herbal SIBO protocols, but the line relies on proprietary blends and skips exact milligrams — a fair ding on transparency. Cross-check it all against your own label.

Active Ingredients

Here are the three bottles and what each one does:

  • K-64 (the antimicrobial) — berberine HCl for gram-negatives and much of the methane crowd, oregano oil for gram-positives, artemisinin from sweet wormwood and magnolia bark to reach the biofilms, plus goldenseal, sage, and supporting herbs
  • K-22 (the binder) — zeolite, activated charcoal, and apple pectin, there to catch die-off toxins before they get re-absorbed
  • K-29 (liver support) — sulforaphane-rich broccoli seed extract with milk thistle, backing up the liver during the protocol
  • Meant to run as a timed course (ramp, full dose, taper), not as separate everyday supplements

Other Ingredients (Excipients)

With GI Synergy the 'what else is in it' question is really about disclosure, because you are looking at a multi-bottle pack rather than one blended capsule. The doses are clinical and line up with the published herbal SIBO protocols, but Apex labels the pieces as proprietary blends without spelling out the milligrams — the honest transparency catch, since you cannot pin down exact amounts from the box. If you are keeping tabs on a particular herb dose or steering around a specific excipient, treat your current pack label as the last word and ask the practitioner channel for the details.

Allergens and Sensitivities

The sensitivities worth watching here are about plants, not the standard food allergens. Goldenseal is the usual suspect behind that rare rash. Artemisinin comes from sweet wormwood, a daisy-family plant, which can matter if ragweed-type allergies are your thing. Oregano oil is potent enough to unsettle a touchy stomach, one reason it goes down with food. The bigger reaction to brace for is not an allergy at all but the die-off, and you manage that by easing the dose up and keeping the binder on schedule — not by steering clear of the product. If plants or capsule fillers tend to bother you, give the label a read first.

How Ingredients Compare to Similar Products

Two comparisons stand out. First, GI Synergy against rifaximin: rifaximin is a non-absorbed prescription antibiotic with the strongest hydrogen-dominant evidence, while GI Synergy is the herbal lane a Johns Hopkins study rated about even for easing symptoms — and the two can be paired, which comes up often for methane-dominant cases. Second, GI Synergy against a lone herb like berberine or oregano on its own: the multi-bottle pack is built for wide coverage plus biofilm disruption plus a binder, which usually edges out a single agent against a settled-in overgrowth, with the trade-off of more pieces and tighter timing. And it helps to name what it is not — the antimicrobial chapter of a SIBO plan, not a one-shot cure, and no replacement for working out what set off the overgrowth.
